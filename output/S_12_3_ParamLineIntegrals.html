Section 1.3 Using Parameterizations to Calculate Line Integrals

¶
Objectives

  • How can we use a parameterization of an oriented curve \(C\) to calculate \(\int_C\vF\cdot d\vr\text{?}\)

  • If a parameterization is used to calculate \(\int_C\vF\cdot d\vr\text{,}\) does the parameterization chosen alter the value of the line integral?

We begin this section by taking a look at how we might go about calculating a line integral of a vector field along some line segments and will use this as inspiration to see how treating oriented curves as vector-valued functions can allow us to quickly turn a line integral of a vector field into an integral of a single variable.

Preview Activity 1.3.1

Let \(\vF=\langle xy,y^2\rangle\text{,}\) let \(C_1\) be the line segment from from \((1,1)\) to \((4,1)\text{,}\) let \(C_2\) be the line segment from \((4,1)\) to \((4,3)\text{,}\) and let \(C_3\) be the line segment from \((1,1)\) to \((4,3)\text{.}\) Also let \(C = C_1 + C_2\text{.}\) This vector field and the curves are shown in Figure 1.3.1.

  1. Every point along \(C_1\) has \(y=1\text{.}\) Therefore, along \(C_1\text{,}\) the vector field \(\vF\) can be viewed purely as a function of \(x\text{.}\) In particular, along \(C_1\text{,}\) we have \(\vF(x,1) = \langle x,1\rangle\text{.}\) Since every point along \(C_2\) has the same \(x\)-value, what (in terms of \(y\) only) is \(\vF\) along \(C_2\text{?}\)

  2. Recall that \(d\vr \approx \Delta \vr\text{,}\) and along \(C_1\text{,}\) we have that \(\Delta\vr = \Delta x\vi \approx dx\vi\text{.}\) Thus, \(d\vr = \langle dx,0\rangle\text{.}\) We know that along \(C_1\text{,}\) \(\vF = \langle x,1\rangle\text{.}\) What does this mean \(\vF\cdot d\vr\) is along \(C_1\text{?}\) What interval of \(x\)-values describes \(C_1\text{?}\) Use these facts fact to write \(\int_{C_1} \vF\cdot d\vr\) as an integral of the form \(\int_a^b f(x)\, dx\) and evaluate the integral.

  3. Use an analogous approach to write \(\int_{C_2} \vF\cdot d\vr\) as a limit of the form \(\int_\alpha^\beta g(y)\, dy\) and evaluate the integral.

  4. Use the previous parts and a property of line integrals to calculate \(\int_C\vF\cdot d\vr\) without having to evaluate any additional integrals.

  5. Evaluating \(\int_{C_3}\vF\cdot d\vr\) takes more work at this stage, so let's break the process into smaller pieces.

    1. Since \(C_3\) is a line segment, find the slope-intercept (\(y=mx+b\)) form of the equation of this line.

    2. Just as we noticed that along \(C_1\) we always had \(y=1\text{,}\) we now know how to express \(y\) in terms of \(x\) for all points along \(C_3\text{.}\) Use this to to express \(\vF(x,y) = \vF(x,mx+b)\) as a vector purely in terms of \(x\) for points along \(C_3\text{.}\)

    3. We often think of the slope of a line as being \(\Delta y/\Delta x\text{.}\) Use this fact and the slope of the line containing \(C_3\) to express \(\Delta y\) as a multiple of \(\Delta x\text{.}\)

    4. We may view \(\Delta\vr\) as \(\langle \Delta x,\Delta y\rangle\text{.}\) Since \(\Delta x\approx dx\) and \(\Delta y\approx dy\text{,}\) write \(d\vr\) as a vector in terms of \(dx\text{.}\)

    5. Use the range of \(x\)-values covered by the line segment \(C_3\) to write \(\int_{C_3}\vF\cdot d\vr\) as a single-variable integral of the form \(\int_a^b f(x)\, dx\) and evaluate the integral.

  6. Notice that \(C\) and \(C_3\) both start at \((1,1)\) and end at \((4,3)\text{.}\) How do the values of \(\int_C\vF\cdot d\vr\) and \(\int_{C_3}\vF\cdot d\vr\) compare?

  7. Is \(\vF\) a gradient vector field? Why or why not?Hint: If \(\vF\) were a gradient vector field, then there would be a function \(f\) such that \(\vF = \nabla f\text{.}\) What would Clairaut's theorem say in this case?

Subsection 1.3.1 Parameterizations in the Definition of \(\int_C\vF\cdot d\vr\)

¶

Preview Activity 1.3.1 has shown us that it is possible to evaluate line integrals without needing to resort to working with Riemann sums directly. However, the approaches taken there seem rather cumbersome to use for oriented curves that are not line segments. Fortunately, parameterizing the oriented curve along which a line integral is calculated provides a powerful tool for evaluating line integrals.

Suppose that \(C\) is an oriented curve traced out by the vector-valued function \(\vr(t)\) for \(a\leq t\leq b\text{,}\) and let \(\vF\) be a continuous vector field defined near \(C\text{.}\) We can divide the interval \([a,b]\) into \(n\) subintervals, each of length \(\Delta t = (b-a)/n\text{,}\) by letting \(t_i = a + i\Delta t\) for \(i = 0,1,\dots,n\text{.}\) This subdivision of \([a,b]\) then can be used to break \(C\) up into \(n\) pieces by letting \(\Delta \vr_i = \vr(t_{i+1}) - \vr(t_{i})\) for \(i=0,1,\dots,n-1\text{.}\) Now notice that

\begin{equation*} \Delta \vr_i = \vr(t_{i+1}) - \vr(t_{i}) = \vr(t_{i} + \Delta t) - \vr(t_{i}) = \frac{\vr(t_{i} + \Delta t) - \vr(t_{i})}{\Delta t}\Delta t\text{.} \end{equation*}

Since the ratio in the last expression for \(\Delta \vr_i\) is approximately \(\vr'(t_{i})\text{,}\) we can say that \(\Delta\vr_i\approx \vr'(t_i)\Delta t\text{.}\) Substituting this into the Reimann sum in the definition of the line integral, we have

\begin{equation*} \int_C \vF\cdot d\vr = \lim_{|\Delta\vr_i|\to 0} \sum_{i=0}^{n-1}\vF(\vr_i)\cdot\Delta\vr_i = \lim_{\Delta t\to 0} \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} \vF(\vr(t_i))\cdot \vr'(t_i)\Delta t\text{.} \end{equation*}

This final Riemann sum is \(\int_a^b \vF(\vr(t))\cdot \vr'(t)\, dt\text{,}\) allowing us to reduce the evaluation of a line integral of a vector-valued function along an oriented curve to an ordinary integral of a function of one variable, since after evaluating the dot product, \(\vF(\vr(t))\cdot \vr'(t)\) is (scalar) function of \(t\text{.}\) We restate this result below for easy reference.

Let \(C\) be a smooth, oriented curve traced out by the vector-valued function \(\vr(t)\) for \(a\leq t\leq b\) and let \(\vF\) be a continuous vector field defined near \(C\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} \int_C \vF\cdot d\vr = \int_a^b \vF(\vr(t))\cdot \vr'(t)\, dt\text{.} \end{equation*}
Example 1.3.2

Let \(\vF(x,y) = x\vi + y^2\vj\) and let \(C\) be the quarter of the circle of radius \(3\) from \((0,3)\) to \((3,0)\text{.}\) This vector field and curve are shown in Figure 1.3.3. By properties of line integrals, we know that \(\int_C \vF\cdot d\vr = -\int_{-C}\vF\cdot d\vr\text{,}\) and we will use this property since \(-C\) is the usual clockwise orientation of a circle, meaning we can parameterize \(-C\) by \(\vr(t) = \langle 3\cos(t),3\sin(t)\rangle\) for \(0\leq t\leq \pi/2\text{.}\)

To evaluate \(\int_{-C}\vF\cdot d\vr\) using this parameterization, we need to note that

\begin{equation*} \vF(\vr(t)) = \langle 3\cos(t) , 9\sin^2(t)\rangle\qquad\text{ and } \vr'(t) = \langle -3\sin(t),3\cos(t)\rangle\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus, we have

\begin{align*} \int_C\vF\cdot d\vr \amp = -\int_{-C}\vF\cdot d\vr = -\int_0^{\pi/2} \langle 3\cos(t),9\sin^2(t)\rangle\cdot\langle -3\sin(t),3\cos(t)\rangle\, dt\\ \amp = -\int_0^{\pi/2} \left(-9\sin(t)\cos(t) + 27\sin^2(t)\cos(t)\right)\, dt\\ \amp = -\int_0^1 (-9 u + 27u^2)\, du = -\left[ -\frac{9}{2}u^2 + 9u^3\right]_0^1 = -\left(-\frac{9}{2} + 9\right) = -\frac{9}{2}\text{.} \end{align*}

Subsection 1.3.2 Alternative Notation for Line Integrals

In contexts where the fact that the quantity we are measuring via a line integral is best measured via a dot product (such as calculating work), the notation we have used thus far for line integrals is fairly common. However, sometimes the vector field is such that the units on \(x\text{,}\) \(y\text{,}\) and \(z\) are not distances. In this case, a dot product may not have a physical meaning, and an alternative notation using differentials can be common. Specifically, if \(\vF(x,y,z) = F_1(x,y,z)\vi + F_2(x,y,z)\vj + F_3(x,y,z)\vk\text{,}\) then

\begin{equation*} \int_C\vF\cdot d\vr = \int_C F_1\, dx + F_2\, dy + F_3\, dz\text{.} \end{equation*}

(If \(\vF\) is a vector field in \(\R^2\text{,}\) the \(F_3\, dz\) term is omitted.) As a concrete example, if \(\vF(x,y,z) = \langle x^2y,z^3,x\cos(z)\rangle\) and \(C\) is some oriented curve in \(\R^3\text{,}\) then

\begin{equation*} \int_C\vF\cdot d\vr = \int_C x^2y\, dx + z^3\, dy + x\cos(z)\, dz\text{.} \end{equation*}

It is important to recognize that the integral on the right-hand side is still a line integral and must be evaluated using techniques for evaluating line integrals. We cannot simply try to treat it as if it were a definite integral of a function of one variable.

Subsection 1.3.3 Independence of Parameterization

Up to this point, we've just been choosing whatever parameterization of an oriented curve \(C\) came to mind, and our argument for how we can use parameterizations to calculate line integrals did not depend on the specific choice of parameterization. However, it is not immediately obvious that different parameterizations don't result in different values of the line integral. Our next example explores this question.

Example 1.3.4

Consider the vector field \(\vF = x\vi\text{.}\) Let us consider two different oriented curves from \((0,1)\) to \((3,3)\text{.}\) The first oriented curve \(C\) travels horizontally to \((3,1)\) and then proceeds vertically to \((3,3)\text{.}\) The second oriented curve \(C_3\) is the line segment from \((0,1)\) to \((3,3)\text{.}\) Notice that, as depicted in Figure 1.3.5, we can break \(C\) up into two oriented curves \(C_1\) (the horizontal portion) and \(C_2\) (the vertical portion) so that \(C = C_1 + C_2\text{.}\)

We first note that since \(\vF\) is orthogonal to \(C_2\text{,}\) \(\int_{C_2}\vF\cdot d\vr=0\text{;}\) threfore \(\int_C\vF\cdot d\vr = \int_{C_1}\vF\cdot d\vr\text{.}\) We can parameterize \(C_1\) as just \(x\vi+\vj\) for \(0\leq x\leq 3\text{,}\) which leads to

\begin{equation*} \int_{C_1}\vF\cdot d\vr = \int_0^3\langle x,0\rangle\cdot \langle 1,0\rangle\, dx = \int_0^3 x\, dx = \frac{9}{2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus, \(\int_C\vF\cdot d\vr = 9/2\text{.}\)

Now we look at \(\int_{C_3}\vF\cdot d\vr\text{,}\) but we parameterize \(C_3\) in a bit of a nonstandard way by letting \(\vr(t) = \langle 3\sin(t),1+2\sin(t)\rangle\) for \(0\leq t\leq \frac{\pi}{2}\text{.}\) Then \(\vr'(t) = \langle 3\cos(t),2\cos(t)\rangle\text{,}\) and

\begin{equation*} \int_{C_3}\vF\cdot d\vr = \int_0^{\pi/2}\langle 3\sin(t),0\rangle\cdot\langle 3\cos(t),2\cos(t)\rangle\, dt = \int_0^{\pi/2} 9\sin(t)\cos(t)\, dt = \frac{9}{2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

In the next activity, you are asked to consider the more typical parameterization of \(C_3\) and verify that using it gives the same value for the line integral.

It's also worth observing here that \(\int_C\vF\cdot d\vr = \int_{C_3}\vF\cdot d\vr\text{,}\) so at least two (very different) paths from \((0,1)\) to \((3,3)\) give the same value of the line integral here. The next section will further investigate this phenomenon and when it happens.

Subsection 1.3.4 Summary

  • If \(C_1\) and \(C_2\) are different paths from \(P\) to \(Q\text{,}\) it is possible for \(\int_{C_1}\vF\cdot d\vr\) to have a different value to \(\int_{C_2}\vF\cdot d\vr\text{.}\)

  • Line integrals of vector fields along oriented curves can be evaluated by parameterizing the curve in terms of \(t\) and then calculating the integral of \(\vF(\vr(t))\cdot \vr'(t)\) on the interval \([a,b]\text{.}\)

  • The line integral \(\int_C\vF\cdot d\vr\) can also be written as \(\int_C F_1\, dx + F_2\, dy\) if \(\vF = \langle F_1,F_2\rangle\) is a vector field in \(\R^2\) or \(\int_C F_1\, dx + F_2\, dy + F_3\, dz\) if \(\vF = \langle F_1,F_2,F_3\rangle\) is a vector field in \(\R^3\text{.}\)
