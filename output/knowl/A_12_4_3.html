Activity 1.4.4

Suppose that \(\vF\) is a continuous path-independent vector field (in \(\R^2\) or \(\R^3\)) on some domain \(D\text{.}\)

  1. Let \(P\) and \(Q\) be points in \(D\) and let \(C_1\) and \(C_2\) be oriented curves from \(P\) to \(Q\text{.}\) What can you say about \(\int_{C_1}\vF\cdot d\vr\) and \(\int_{C_2}\vF\cdot d\vr\text{?}\)

  2. Let \(C = C_1 - C_2\text{.}\) Explain why \(C\) is a closed curve.

  3. Calculate \(\oint_C\vF\cdot d\vr\text{.}\) (Recall that we sometimes use the symbol \(\oint\) for a line integral when the curve is closed.)

  4. What does the previous part show must be the value of \(\oint_C \vF\cdot d\vr\) for any closed curve \(C\) and continuous path-independent vector field \(\vF\text{?}\) \saveCount

From the first part of this activity, you now now that the line integral around any closed curve in a path-independent vector field is \(0\text{.}\) What can we say about the converse? That is, suppose that \(\vF\) is a continuous vector field on a domain \(D\) for which \(\oint_C\vF\cdot d\vr = 0\) for all closed curves \(C\text{.}\)

  1. Pick two points \(P\) and \(Q\) in \(D\text{.}\) Let \(C_1\) and \(C_2\) be oriented curves from \(P\) to \(Q\text{.}\) What type of curve is \(C = C_1 - C_2\text{?}\)

  2. What is \(\oint_C\vF\cdot d\vr\text{?}\) Why?

  3. What does that tell you about the relationship between \(\int_{C_1}\vF\cdot d\vr\) and \(\int_{C_2}\vF\cdot d\vr\text{?}\)

  4. Explain why this shows that \(\vF\) is path-independent.
